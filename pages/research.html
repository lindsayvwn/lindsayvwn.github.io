University of St. Andrews

First Class Disseration: Anthropogenic noise and bird vocalisations[sic] : an attempt to classically condition UK garden birds to exhibit the Lombard effect

Abstract: Humans produce a substantial amount of anthropogenic noise, which can impair or mask the vocal communications of many species. Some species compensate for anthropogenic noise by increasing their call amplitude (the Lombard effect); however, this adaptive response is energetically costly. Species could preserve energy if they could learn to associate an auditory cue with an increase in anthropogenic noise (i.e classical conditioning). This study investigated whether UK garden birds could be classically conditioned to exhibit the Lombard effect by associating a cue (a beep) with anthropogenic noise. Conditioning took place at five sites over a period of two weeks, followed by the Testing Day, when the beep was played without anthropogenic noise. The birds were not classically conditioned, nor did they exhibit the Lombard effect on the first and last conditioning days; however, birds delayed calling after the beep. These findings suggest that birds cannot learn cue association, but the delay in call latency after the beep may indicate that the Lombard effect is an immediate response that does not use cue association. A better understanding of how birds adjust their calls in the presence of anthropogenic noise can provide insight into how these species adapt to an increasingly noisier world.
